The Community

Who lives here. How they join. What binds them together.

The People

Who Makes Up the Community

Four groups come together on this campus — each with a distinct role, each essential to the ecosystem.

Learners

Gunagrahis

Young learners enrolled in the KarmYog GunaKul, ages 6 to 16. They live on campus in residential dormitories — immersed in a daily rhythm of discipline, creativity, reflection, and community life.

Separate Gunagrahi Awaas for boys and girls ensure dignity and safety. Each Awaas houses 12 learners.

12 beds per Awaas · 36 per ashram · 144 across campus

Elders

Swajans

Elder residents, age 50 and above. These are not passive residents — they mentor, share stories, offer wisdom, and participate actively in the life of the campus. Swajan means "our own people."

Each Swajan lives in a dignified community home with lifetime residence — a place of belonging, not just shelter.

7 Swajan Grihas per ashram · 28 across campus

Visitors

Visiting Acharyas & Guests

Scholars, Gurus, and institutional visitors who stay in Atithi Kutirs — guest accommodations designed for reflection and exchange. Their presence enriches the campus with fresh knowledge and perspective.

Values alignment is required. Not every guest is admitted — the institution ensures compatibility with the community's ethos.

Supporters

Sponsors

Contributors who fund the construction and sustenance of the campus. Sponsors receive naming rights and nomination rights — the ability to nominate a participant for the community.

A sponsor may or may not be a participant themselves. The roles are distinct by design.

Admissions

How Participants Join

GunaKul Admissions: Selection is based on curiosity, aptitude, discipline, attitude, family alignment, and institutional fit — not academic marks alone. The GunaKul seeks learners who will thrive in an immersive community, not just perform in exams.

Swajan Admissions: Willingness to participate in community life, compatibility with other residents, dignity, and health suitability are the criteria. This is a community of active elders, not a care facility.

Institutional Discretion: The institution reserves the right to decline admission without public explanation — this is a safeguard for community protection, exercised in good faith.

Sponsor Nominations: Sponsors can nominate themselves, their children, their parents, or anyone they choose — subject to the admissions criteria above.

Rights & Responsibilities

Nomination & Sponsorship Rights

A critical distinction: Sponsor is not the same as Participant. A sponsor funds a space. A participant lives in or uses that space. These roles may overlap, but they are designed to be separate.

Sponsor Type Nomination Right
Swajan Griha Sponsor Can nominate an elder (50+) for lifetime residence — including themselves
Gunagrahi Awaas Sponsor First right of refusal on beds — can nominate their children (ages 6-16) or any learner
Self-Nomination Sponsors aged 50+ can nominate themselves as Swajan residents
On Exit / Demise Up to 50% refund of sponsorship, subject to replacement onboarding by the institution

All nominations are subject to the institution's admissions criteria. Sponsorship guarantees a right to nominate, not automatic admission.

Living Together

Daily Life & Community Principles

Life on the campus follows a rhythm — not rigid, but intentional. Reflection, participation, care for shared spaces, and the discipline of community living form the foundation of each day.

Community meals in the Bhojanalaya are central to campus life — rooted in health, simplicity, and the practice of shared living. Everyone eats together.

Everyone contributes. The elders offer wisdom, stories, and mentorship. The learners bring energy, curiosity, and digital fluency. Staff, visitors, and volunteers bring care and discipline. No one is merely a consumer of this community.

The campus operates under the tutelage of MahAcharyaJi and GunaMata — the institutional guardians who set the tone, resolve conflicts, and hold the community to its charter.

Protection

Safety & Safeguarding

A community with children and elders demands the highest standards of protection. These are non-negotiable.

  • Zero tolerance for abuse of children — reporting mechanisms, mandatory background checks for all staff and long-term visitors, and designated safeguarding officers on campus at all times
  • Elder protection — safeguards against exploitation, humiliation, and neglect. Every Swajan is treated with the dignity their years have earned
  • Code of conduct — respect for all residents, no harassment, no substance abuse, no proselytizing. Violations lead to institutional action, including removal
  • Technology discipline for learners — structured rules for phones, devices, and internet access. Technology serves learning; it does not replace it

The institution maintains the right to enforce these standards without compromise. Community safety is not subject to negotiation.

Governance

The Community Charter

The Riverside Vatika Community Charter is the working constitution for residents, learners, sponsors, staff, visitors, and institutional participants. It covers admissions, daily conduct, rights and responsibilities, safeguarding, grievance redressal, and the principles that hold this community together.

"The Community Charter is a living document. It may be amended by the Institution in good faith, as the community grows and learns from its own experience. It is not a contract — it is a covenant."

The full charter is available on request. It is shared with every sponsor and participant before they join the community, in English, Hindi and Bengali.
